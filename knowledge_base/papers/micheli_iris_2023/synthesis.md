## TL;DR
IRIS is a model-based RL agent built on a **discrete autoencoder + autoregressive Transformer** world model: frames are tokenised into a small grid of discrete tokens by a VQ-VAE; a GPT-style Transformer treats interleaved (obs-tokens, action, reward, done) sequences as a single autoregressive language; an actor-critic learns purely from rolled-out imagined token sequences decoded back to images. With only ~2 hours of gameplay (Atari-100k = 400k frames at action-repeat 4 = 100k env steps), IRIS achieves **mean human-normalised score of 1.046** across 26 Atari games — first method without lookahead search to beat humans on average at this budget. Replaces the Dreamer-family RSSM/GRU stack with pure attention; demonstrates that sequence-modelling priors (Transformers) work as world-model backbones, not just policy backbones (Decision Transformer).

## Problem framing
Two pre-existing strands: (a) Dreamer-family RSSMs (recurrent latents, continuous + categorical) excel at long-horizon imagination but are bespoke architectures; (b) Decision Transformer / Trajectory Transformer use sequence models *to act* but not as full world models supporting imagination training. IRIS asks: can the off-the-shelf Transformer language-model recipe — discrete tokens, causal attention, NLL training — serve as the imagination engine itself? Builds on VQ-VAE (van den Oord 2017), GPT-2 (Radford 2019), Dreamer (Hafner), and SimPLe / MuZero on Atari-100k.

## Method
**Tokeniser (V).** A VQ-VAE encodes each 64×64 RGB Atari frame to a **4×4 grid of 16 discrete tokens** from a codebook of size K=512. Trained with reconstruction + commitment + codebook losses. Decoder reconstructs the frame.

**Dynamics model (G).** A GPT-style decoder-only Transformer. Tokens at each timestep are arranged as: [o_t^{(1)}, o_t^{(2)}, …, o_t^{(16)}, a_t, r_t, d_t]. The Transformer is trained next-token-prediction style to predict subsequent obs-tokens (cross-entropy over codebook), next reward (regression / discretised), and next done (binary). Architecture in the paper: 10 layers, 4 heads, d_model=256, context length ~20 timesteps × 17 tokens-per-step.

**Actor-Critic (π, V).** Lightweight CNN-MLP. Trained on **imagined rollouts** generated by G: starting from a real observation's tokens, autoregressively sample H=15-20 future steps in token space, decode every step's frame via V's decoder, run π and V over decoded frames. Actor uses REINFORCE with a learned baseline; critic is λ-return TD. Training alternates between (a) gathering real env data, (b) training V on real frames, (c) training G on the replay buffer, (d) training π,V on imagined trajectories.

**Imagination horizon** H=15; batch sizes modest (256 sequences × 20 steps) — full training run is ~1 week on 1 GPU.

## Key results
**Atari-100k (26 games, 2-hour budget):**
- IRIS mean HNS = **1.046** (median ~0.29), beating SimPLe (0.13), CURL (0.26), DrQ (0.27), SPR (0.62), EfficientZero (1.95-mean / 1.16-median uses MCTS so not strictly comparable).
- IRIS is the **best non-lookahead method** at this budget.
- Per-game: wins on Breakout (84 → 84), Boxing (70+), Krull, Demon Attack; struggles on Asterix, Frostbite, Gravitar (hard exploration).

**Crafter**: the original IRIS paper does *not* evaluate on Crafter. Follow-up work and the related DIAMOND (Alonso et al. 2024) and Δ-IRIS extensions have begun to address Crafter-style envs, but the canonical IRIS submission is Atari-100k only. We should treat any Crafter score attributed to IRIS as a follow-up, not the original paper.

**Ablations**: 
- Token grid size 4×4 vs 8×8: smaller grids hurt reconstruction but speed training; chosen as compute compromise.
- Imagination horizon H: monotone gain up to ~15; flat beyond.
- Removing the imagination training stage (real-data-only AC) collapses performance to SimPLe-level.

## Limitations
- **Compute**: ~1 week / GPU per game (no shared multi-task training).
- **Atari only** in the original paper; generalisation to 3D / continuous-control unclear (later work extends).
- **Token grid loses spatial fidelity**: 16 tokens for 64×64 frame is coarse; some games have small critical sprites that disappear under VQ.
- **No long-term memory** mechanism beyond the Transformer context window (~20 steps).
- **Sample-efficient ≠ asymptotic**: IRIS at 100k is great; the paper doesn't show it scales as well at 200M frames vs Dreamer/MuZero.
- The actor-critic is feed-forward over decoded frames — could itself be a bottleneck given the tokenised world model's expressive imagination.

## Idea seeds for our loop
- **Bar to beat on Crafter-100k**: IRIS doesn't report it, but the Atari-100k → Crafter-100k follow-ups (DIAMOND, Δ-IRIS) set scores in the 10-25% range at the 100k budget that we should track. **
- **Discrete-token world model + iterative-PC inference**: the VQ tokeniser is amortised; what if we *iterate* the token assignments (settle z) given top-down context? PC-style inference of the discrete codes — similar to MAP inference in VAE-with-categorical — might give better reconstructions at no extra parameters. Concrete: replace V's encoder with a PC settle that minimises both reconstruction error and dynamics-model NLL.
- **Transformers + PC**: the dynamics Transformer is a frozen amortised function f(history) → next-token logits. PC could replace this with energy minimisation over a context window, where the latent token sequence is iteratively refined by a sequence-energy. Risk: prohibitively expensive without a clever message-passing scheme.
- **Actor-side iteration**: imagined trajectories give us 15 steps × 16 tokens × frame-decode of "free" inference compute per real step. An iterative-inference policy refined on the imagined rollout is a clean drop-in beating IRIS's feed-forward AC on the same world model. **
- **Replace VQ with a Helmholtz-machine / PC encoder-decoder pair**, giving us continuous latents that can be settled to arbitrary precision, vs discrete tokens locked to the codebook. Question: does the discreteness *help* (cleaner imagination) or *hurt* (info bottleneck)?
- **Imagination horizon × iteration depth interaction**: H=15 for IRIS, settle_K=N for us — the cross of these two knobs is the relevant 2D sweep. Hypothesis: short H with large settle_K matches long H with small settle_K, but with better wall-clock.
